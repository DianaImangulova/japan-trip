Meiji Shrine



Meiji Shrine is Tokyo's most important Shinto shrine, dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken. Nestled in a 175-acre forest in the heart of Tokyo, this peaceful sanctuary offers a spiritual escape from the bustling city.

Built in 1920, the shrine is surrounded by over 100,000 trees donated from all over Japan. It's one of the most popular places for traditional Japanese wedding ceremonies and New Year's prayers, attracting millions of visitors annually.

  • üìç Location: 1-1 Yoyogikamizonocho, Shibuya-ku, Tokyo
  • üïê Opening hours: 6:10 AM - 4:10 PM (in November)
  • üí∞ Admission: Free (Inner Garden: ¬•500)
  • üöá Station: Harajuku Station (JR Yamanote Line) or Meiji-jingu-mae Station

üëë Emperor Meiji Legacy

Emperor Meiji (1852-1912) led Japan's transformation from a feudal society to a modern nation. The shrine honors his role in the Meiji Restoration, which opened Japan to the world and modernized the country.

üå≥ Sacred Forest

The forest was artificially created from donated trees from across Japan and around the world. Today it's home to over 120 species of trees and serves as a green lung in central Tokyo.

üéã Ema Wish Plaques

Write your wishes on wooden ema plaques and hang them at the shrine. Popular wishes include good health, success in studies, and finding love. The shrine receives thousands of ema daily.

üíí Traditional Weddings

One of Japan's most popular wedding venues. You might witness traditional Shinto wedding processions with brides in beautiful white shiromuku kimono and grooms in formal hakama.

üéå New Year Hatsumode

Japan's most popular shrine for New Year's first prayer (hatsumode), attracting over 3 million visitors during the first three days of January. The crowds are massive but the atmosphere is magical.

Harajuku & Takeshita Street



Harajuku is Tokyo's epicenter of youth culture and fashion creativity. From kawaii (cute) culture to cosplay, this vibrant district showcases Japan's most innovative and colorful street styles.

Takeshita Street is the heart of Harajuku - a narrow, pedestrian-only street packed with trendy shops, quirky cafes, and unique fashion boutiques. It's where Tokyo's teenage fashion trends are born and where creativity knows no bounds.

üåà Kawaii Culture

"Kawaii" (cute) culture originated in Harajuku and spread worldwide. It's not just about looking cute - it's a lifestyle celebrating innocence, playfulness, and rejecting traditional adult seriousness.

üëò Fashion Subcultures

Harajuku is home to unique fashion styles: Decora (colorful accessories), Gothic Lolita (Victorian-inspired), Fairy Kei (pastel fantasy), and Visual Kei (dramatic makeup and clothes).

üì± Instagram Paradise

Every corner of Takeshita Street is designed for social media. From neon signs to rainbow foods, the entire area is crafted to create the perfect Instagram shot.

üé≠ Street Performers

On weekends, talented street performers entertain crowds with music, dance, and cosplay. Many famous Japanese pop stars got their start performing on Harajuku streets.

Purikura Photo Booth Fun



Purikura (print club) are decorated photo booths that have been a Japanese obsession since the 1990s. These high-tech machines take photos and let you edit them with digital decorations, creating uniquely Japanese keepsakes.

In Harajuku, purikura arcades are everywhere, offering the latest machines with advanced beauty filters, AR effects, and endless customization options. It's a quintessential Tokyo youth culture experience!

üì∏ How to Use Purikura

Step 1: Choose Your Machine

Pick from hundreds of different themed machines. Popular ones have longer queues but offer the latest effects.

Step 2: Take Photos (3-5 minutes)

Strike poses and follow the machine's instructions. It usually takes 16 photos automatically with different backgrounds.

Step 3: Edit & Decorate (10 minutes)

Add text, stamps, frames, and digital makeup. This is the fun creative part where you can go wild!

Step 4: Print & Share

Collect your printed stickers and save digital copies to your phone via QR code.

Pro Tips:
  • Bring coins - most machines only accept cash
  • Groups of 2-4 work best
  • Don't rush the editing phase - it's the most fun part!
  • Popular machines in Takeshita Street: Big Bang, Love Berry, Princess Deco

Shibuya Crossing & Shibuya Sky



Shibuya Crossing is the world's busiest pedestrian intersection, where up to 3,000 people cross simultaneously during rush hour. This iconic scramble crossing has become a symbol of Tokyo's energy and urban intensity.

Shibuya Sky is the newest observation deck offering stunning 360-degree views of Tokyo from 230 meters high. The rooftop experience provides the perfect aerial view of the famous crossing below and Tokyo's endless skyline.

üö∂ The Crossing Experience

Best experienced during rush hours (8-9 AM, 6-7 PM) when up to 3,000 people cross simultaneously. The organized chaos is mesmerizing - despite the crowds, people rarely bump into each other.

üêï Hachiko Memorial

Visit the statue of Hachiko, the loyal dog who waited for his deceased owner every day for 10 years. This meeting spot has become a symbol of loyalty and love in Japanese culture.

üì∫ Center Gai & Shopping

Explore Center Gai, Shibuya's vibrant shopping street filled with fashion stores, karaoke boxes, game arcades, and restaurants. It's where Tokyo's youth culture comes alive.
